    AI and English Dialogic Learning: Key Takeaways from a Conference on Technology in Higher Education#

    Based on a conference presentation by Do Phuong Anh, 2025

    At the Faculty of English for Specific Purposes conference at Foreign Trade University, a presentation synthesized current research on integrating AI into English dialogic learning for higher education contexts. The session drew together two of the most consequential trends in education today: the global demand for English language proficiency and the rapid rise of Artificial Intelligence. What follows is a summary of the key arguments and evidence presented.

    The Starting Point: Two Converging Trends#

    Modern higher education is shaped by two forces that don’t always communicate with each other. On one side, globalization has made English proficiency a near-universal professional requirement. On the other, AI has become sophisticated enough to engage in meaningful language interaction. The question the presentation asked—and that the field is beginning to answer—is what happens when these trends intersect in the English language classroom.

    The historical arc of English language learning is relevant here. The field moved from grammar-translation approaches toward communicative paradigms precisely because researchers recognized that language acquisition is a social, interactive process [1]. Vygotsky’s [2] insight that meaning-making is fundamentally collaborative set the theoretical foundation that dialogic learning builds on. Now AI is offering a new kind of collaborative partner—and the teaching profession needs to know what to do with it.

    AI’s Capabilities That Matter for Dialogic Learning#

    Three capabilities of modern AI align particularly well with dialogic learning’s demands:

    Natural Language Processing: AI can understand and generate human language with increasing sophistication, making conversational interaction possible in ways that earlier educational technology couldn’t achieve.

    Personalization: AI systems can adapt content, pacing, and feedback to individual learner characteristics in real time—something human teachers struggle to do consistently across diverse classrooms.

    Adaptive Feedback: Rather than waiting days for graded assignments, learners receive immediate, context-sensitive responses that allow them to adjust in the moment [3].

    These capabilities collectively enable what foundational AI models like Large Language Models are increasingly seen as providing: personalized, engaging, and scalable interactive learning experiences [3].

    The Six Roles Framework#

    Drawing on Pavlova [4], the presentation outlined six distinct roles AI can occupy in the dialogic learning ecosystem:

    RoleWhat AI Does
    Possibility EngineGenerates alternative expressions, expanding linguistic range
    Socratic OpponentChallenges arguments, develops critical and persuasive language skills
    Collaboration CoachSupports group problem-solving and collaborative dialogue
    Personal TutorProvides individualized instruction and immediate feedback
    Study BuddyFacilitates reflective dialogue about learning material
    MotivatorOffers interactive challenges to extend engagement

    The significance of this framework is that it reveals AI’s potential for different degrees of agency. A study buddy occupies a supportive, subordinate role; a Socratic opponent challenges and pushes back. Educators choosing how to deploy AI need to be deliberate about which role fits their pedagogical goals—because each role creates different dynamics for the human-AI dialogue and different incentives for learner participation.

    Three Core Benefits, With Evidence#

    Benefit 1: Radical Personalization#

    AI can tailor educational content, pace, and interaction style to individual students in real time—creating customized learning journeys that ensure material is challenging but achievable [5]. For language learners at different proficiency levels sharing the same classroom, this is not a luxury but a pedagogical necessity. Dialogic learning that adapts dynamically to each learner’s contributions and capabilities is categorically different from one-size-fits-all instruction.

    Benefit 2: Boosting Motivation and Confidence#

    The psychological dimension of AI integration turns out to be one of its most important practical contributions. Learners report significantly less communication apprehension when practicing with AI than with human interlocutors [4]. Studies document improvements in EFL learners’ self-confidence, self-regulation, well-being, and L2 motivation when using AI-mediated language practice [6]. AI has been shown to specifically reduce the fear of speaking in English—one of the most persistent obstacles to language development [7].

    For dialogic learning, where authentic participation requires the willingness to take communicative risks, this reduction in anxiety isn’t just a side benefit. It’s what makes dialogue possible at all for many learners.

    Benefit 3: Immediate Feedback and Critical Thinking Development#

    AI chatbots provide immediate feedback that allows learners to identify errors before they become ingrained habits [8]. LLMs can deliver context-specific feedback that goes beyond right/wrong judgments, facilitating exploration of nuance and clarification of misunderstandings [9].

    Crucially, AI can also be designed to foster deeper thinking rather than replace it. When AI asks probing, open-ended questions rather than providing direct answers, it pushes learners toward higher-order reasoning [10]. The “flipped dialogic” approach—where students critique AI-generated solutions rather than receiving AI as the authority—keeps critical work where it belongs: with the human learner [4].

    A Critical Consideration: Context Is King#

    The presentation’s most distinctive contribution may have been its emphasis on context-sensitivity—a point often overlooked in discussions of AI’s educational potential.

    Current AI models largely reflect dominant (and often Western, English-centric) perspectives and assumptions [11]. But effective dialogic learning is culturally dependent. The communication styles, rhetorical conventions, and pedagogical goals that make dialogue authentic in one setting may not translate meaningfully to another. A tool that works brilliantly in one cultural and institutional context may be less appropriate—or even counterproductive—in another.

    The recommendation: move toward what researchers call “grassroots and context-sensitive AI” [12]—tools that can be co-designed with local educators, adapted to local communicative norms, and evaluated against local pedagogical goals. This is a significant design challenge, but it’s the right challenge to take on if AI-enhanced dialogic learning is to be genuinely inclusive.

    Three Recommendations for Responsible Integration#

    1. Develop Robust Ethical Guidelines

    Transparency, fairness, accountability, and inclusivity must be designed into AI educational tools from the start, not added as afterthoughts [13]. Existing guidelines need revision; new ones specific to language education contexts need to be developed and adopted.

    2. Integrate AI with Sound Pedagogy and Teacher Expertise

    Technology should complement and augment teaching, not substitute for it [8]. Co-design processes that bring educators, learners, and policy advisors into AI development decisions are essential for ensuring tools serve genuine educational needs [13].

    3. Empower Users with AI Literacy

    Students need to develop the ability to critically assess AI-generated content, understanding its capabilities and limitations [12]. Teachers need professional development that goes beyond technical training to encompass pedagogical design for AI-enhanced learning environments.

    The Essential Conclusion#

    AI holds real transformative potential for English dialogic learning in higher education. But the session’s conclusion was deliberately measured: AI should be understood as a powerful partner, not a replacement. The human teacher remains indispensable—for the higher-order pedagogical functions that AI cannot perform, for the cultural sensitivity that AI currently lacks, and for the ethical judgment that all educational decisions require.

    The most valuable thing AI can do for dialogic learning is make the conversation richer. The most valuable thing educators can do is ensure that human voices remain at its center.
