    Integrating AI in English Dialogic Learning: Roles, Benefits, and a Human-Centered Path Forward#

    Based on research by Do Phuong Anh, 2025

    The quest for English language proficiency has always driven pedagogical innovation. Dialogic learning—which treats language acquisition as a fundamentally social, collaborative process—has emerged as one of the most promising frameworks for developing genuine communicative competence [1]. Now, the rapid advance of Artificial Intelligence is reshaping what dialogic learning can look like, who (or what) can participate in it, and how educators should position themselves within it.

    This article provides a brief but grounded review of how AI is currently being integrated into English dialogic learning, and what that integration means in practice—for the benefits it delivers, the limitations it reveals, and the decisions it demands of educators [2].

    The Theoretical Foundation: Dialogue as the Engine of Language Learning#

    The theoretical underpinnings of this work draw from several traditions. Vygotsky’s [3] social constructivism established language acquisition as an active process of meaning-making and social interaction—not the memorization of rules. This was formalized into communicative competence frameworks that prioritize authentic interaction over linguistic accuracy alone [4].

    Laurillard’s Conversational Framework [5] provides particular insight into how AI can participate in learning dialogue. The framework describes learning as an iterative cycle: a teacher sets a goal and describes a concept; the learner acts and describes their understanding; the teacher re-describes in light of the learner’s response. AI systems designed for dialogic learning can explicitly embody these conversational turns, enabling both task completion and metacognitive reflection on the language learning process itself.

    Seven Roles AI Can Play in Dialogic Learning#

    Rather than treating AI as a single, monolithic tool, Pavlova [6] identifies seven distinct roles that AI can play in the dialogic learning ecosystem—each with different implications for the power dynamics of human-AI dialogue:

    Possibility Engine: Generates alternative ways of expressing an idea, exposing learners to linguistic diversity and creativity.

    Socratic Opponent: Acts as a challenger, prompting learners to develop and defend arguments—honing critical thinking and persuasive language skills.

    Collaboration Coach: Assists groups in researching topics and solving problems together, facilitating collaborative dialogue.

    Guide on the Side: Helps learners navigate complex conceptual or linguistic territory, offering guidance as needed without dominating the process.

    Personal Tutor: Provides individualized instruction, explanations, and immediate feedback based on each learner’s progress and specific needs.

    Study Buddy: Engages learners in reflective dialogue about learning material, helping them consolidate knowledge and identify gaps.

    Motivator: Offers games, challenges, and interactive scenarios to extend learning and sustain engagement.

    The spectrum of these roles reveals that AI can range from a supportive, subordinate partner (study buddy) to an intellectually challenging peer (Socratic opponent). The pedagogical decision of which role(s) AI embodies will profoundly shape how learners perceive and interact with it—and how willing they are to take the linguistic risks that genuine dialogue requires.

    Three Core Benefits#

    1. Radical Personalization#

    Perhaps AI’s most significant contribution to education is its capacity to personalize. AI systems can tailor educational content, pacing, and interaction styles to meet individual learners’ needs, preferences, and proficiency levels in real time [7]. Adaptive learning technologies analyze performance data continuously to create customized learning journeys—ensuring material is challenging but not overwhelming [8].

    For dialogic learning specifically, this means conversations that adapt dynamically to the learner’s contributions, emerging language capabilities, and comprehension level. The ideal of personalized dialogic instruction—long constrained by high teacher-student ratios—becomes achievable at scale.

    2. Boosting Motivation and Confidence#

    AI-driven tools have demonstrated strong potential to enhance engagement and motivation [8]. LLM-based chatbots have been associated with increased learner motivation and self-efficacy [9]. Studies on AI-mediated language learning report significant improvements in EFL learners’ self-confidence, self-regulation, well-being, and L2 motivation [10].

    A key mechanism is the reduction of communication apprehension. When interacting with AI, learners report less anxiety than in human-to-human exchanges [6]. AI tools have specifically been shown to reduce the fear of speaking in English—one of the most persistent barriers to language development [11]. This psychological safety isn’t just pleasant; it’s pedagogically functional, creating the conditions under which genuine dialogue can occur.

    3. Immediate Feedback and Deeper Thinking#

    AI chatbots and intelligent tutoring systems excel at providing immediate, context-specific feedback [12]. Unlike feedback that arrives days after an assignment is submitted, AI responses allow students to identify errors and adjust their understanding in the moment. LLMs, in particular, can deliver feedback that goes beyond simple right/wrong judgments, facilitating richer dialogic exchanges where learners explore nuances and clarify misunderstandings [13].

    AI can also be designed to promote deeper thinking. The “flipped dialogic learning” approach has students critically review AI-generated solutions, identify potential flaws, and reformulate their questions—actively engaging critical thinking rather than passively receiving information [6]. AI chatbots programmed to ask probing, open-ended questions push learners beyond surface recall toward more robust conceptual understanding [14].

    A Critical Consideration: When AI Over-Scaffolds#

    There’s a genuine tension here that deserves acknowledgment. If AI systems provide answers too readily or scaffold the learning process too heavily, they may inadvertently hinder the development of problem-solving skills and independent critical thought. Dialogic learning ultimately aims to develop independent thinkers [15]—and a personal tutor that’s always available risks becoming a crutch.

    The flipped dialogic learning approach [6] is one pedagogical strategy to mitigate this: instead of having AI answer questions, students are asked to critique AI-generated answers, maintaining the intellectual work on the human side. The AI becomes a stimulus for thinking rather than a substitute for it.

    Three Recommendations for Responsible Integration#

    Develop Robust Ethical Guidelines#

    The rapid advance of AI demands equally rapid development of ethical frameworks—ones that prioritize transparency, fairness, accountability, and inclusivity [16]. Existing guidelines need review and update; specific principles for responsible AI use in ELT remain to be established and widely adopted [17].

    Integrate AI with Sound Pedagogical Principles and Teacher Expertise#

    Technology should augment, not replace, human educators [12]. Successful AI integration requires alignment with pedagogical principles and significant teacher oversight. Co-design processes that involve educators, learners, and policy advisors in the development of AI tools are crucial for ensuring these technologies meet genuine educational needs [16].

    A particularly important direction: developing AI tools that are context-sensitive and locally adaptable. Current AI models often reflect dominant (typically Western, English-centric) perspectives [18]. Effective dialogic learning is highly culturally dependent—influenced by local communication styles and educational goals. Generic AI tools may not resonate or be appropriate across diverse educational settings.

    Empower Learners and Educators with AI Literacy#

    Both groups need to understand what AI can and cannot do. For students, this means developing the ability to critically assess AI-generated content and understand the system’s limitations [17]. For educators, continuous professional development should go beyond basic technical skills to cover AI-enhanced activity design, interpreting AI-generated data, and navigating ethical considerations [19].

    The Bottom Line#

    AI holds transformative potential for English dialogic learning—through personalization, motivational support, and feedback that would be impossible for a single teacher to provide across a classroom of thirty learners. But realizing this potential requires more than deploying tools. It requires a pedagogically sound, human-centered approach that keeps the teacher in the role of intellectual guide, treats AI as a participant rather than a replacement, and builds the ethical frameworks and literacy skills that allow both learners and educators to engage with these technologies critically and responsibly.

    The goal isn’t a future where AI conducts dialogue while humans observe. It’s a future where human-AI partnerships expand what’s possible in dialogic learning—making authentic communicative practice more accessible, more personalized, and more likely to develop the competence that learners actually need.
