    🗣️ How English Language Teachers Perceive AI-Facilitated Dialogic Learning: Benefits, Concerns, and a New Framework

    July 25, 2025

    How English Language Teachers Perceive AI-Facilitated Dialogic Learning: Benefits, Concerns, and a New Framework#

    Based on research by Do Phuong Anh, 2025

    As AI tools permeate every corner of professional life, English language teaching faces a particular moment of reckoning. The core of communicative language pedagogy has always been dialogue—real, messy, unpredictable human exchange. Now a technology capable of engaging in that exchange is arriving in classrooms worldwide. What do teachers actually think about it?

    This report, presented at the Faculty of English for Specific Purposes conference at Foreign Trade University, synthesizes existing literature on English language teachers’ perceptions of AI-facilitated dialogic learning. It introduces a novel conceptual framework—positioning AI not as a tool, but as a dialogic agent—and maps the landscape of teacher attitudes with unusual specificity [1].

    A New Conceptual Framework: AI as Dialogic Agent#

    The central theoretical contribution of this paper is reframing how we think about AI’s role in dialogic learning. Traditional dialogic learning, as formalized by González et al. [2], involves collaborative knowledge construction through shared reflection—a fundamentally human enterprise where teachers facilitate equitable speaking opportunities and critical reasoning.

    The paper proposes that Generative AI can be conceptualized as a dialogic agent: a participant capable of co-constructing knowledge with students through collaborative dialogue, not merely delivering content to them [3]. This framing challenges the assumption that dialogic learning requires human-to-human interaction. Instead, it opens space for human-AI interaction as a legitimate pedagogical form.

    This matters because it changes what we’re assessing when we ask whether AI “works” in the classroom. The question isn’t just whether AI tools improve test scores—it’s whether they can function as genuine participants in the dialogic construction of knowledge.

    What Teachers Are Generally Feeling: Cautious Optimism#

    The synthesis of existing literature finds that EFL teachers generally hold positive perceptions toward AI tools [4]. Many “enthusiastically embrace AI integration” and express willingness to incorporate AI into their lessons because they perceive it as useful [5].

    One counterintuitive finding: teachers with more extensive experience (over 10 years) tend to exhibit more open and positive attitudes toward AI than those with fewer than five years of experience [6]. This challenges the assumption that younger “digital native” teachers would be naturally more receptive. It suggests that pedagogical wisdom and a broader view of educational evolution—rather than general tech-savviness—may be what drives openness to AI.

    Pre-service English teachers (PSETs) also generally report positive experiences, particularly valuing AI’s efficiency and feedback capabilities [7].

    Five Perceived Benefits#

    1. Personalization of Learning#

    The benefit teachers cite most enthusiastically is AI’s capacity to tailor learning experiences to individual needs—adjusting content, pace, and difficulty levels for each student [8]. AI can also offer prompt, context-specific, and individualized feedback on language skills in ways that are difficult for teachers to provide consistently in large classes [9].

    This directly addresses one of ELT’s most persistent challenges: the gap between the ideal of personalized instruction and the reality of high teacher-student ratios [10].

    2. Reduction in Teacher Workload#

    Teachers see significant practical value in AI handling administrative and preparatory tasks—creating materials, grading routine assignments, providing initial feedback. This frees time and cognitive energy for higher-order pedagogical work: guiding complex discussions, building student relationships, providing nuanced support [9].

    3. Specific Skills Development Through Dialogue#

    AI tools like chatbots and speech recognition software provide platforms for students to practice speaking—improving pronunciation, fluency, and confidence—in low-pressure, interactive environments [9]. AI can also facilitate dialogic feedback on student writing, encouraging iterative revision cycles [11].

    4. Fostering Student Autonomy#

    Some teachers are looking beyond efficiency and exploring AI’s potential for developing autonomous, critical learners. The approach: teach students to use AI responsibly for brainstorming, copyediting (while maintaining their own voice), and seeking targeted feedback—without letting AI do the thinking for them [12]. This represents a sophisticated pedagogical strategy, positioning AI not as a shortcut but as a scaffold for building student agency.

    5. Creating a Safe Space for Practice#

    Perhaps the most relevant benefit for dialogic learning specifically: AI creates a psychologically safe environment for language practice. True dialogue requires participants to feel secure enough to express uncertain or “wrong” ideas without fear of judgment [13]. L2 learners often experience significant anxiety when speaking or making errors. AI chatbots provide a “low-pressure environment” [10] where AI-generated feedback can be less embarrassing than direct correction in front of peers [9].

    This psychological safety could serve as a scaffold—reducing anxiety enough that students become willing to engage in the more demanding human-to-human dialogues that develop full communicative competence.

    Four Categories of Concern#

    Technical and Infrastructural Limitations#

    A primary concern is inadequate infrastructure: unreliable internet access, insufficient computers, and compatibility issues with existing school technology [4]. These aren’t minor inconveniences—they directly determine whether AI integration is possible at all in many classroom contexts.

    Accuracy, Credibility, and Ethics#

    Teachers report significant anxiety about AI accuracy: tools that produce incorrect answers, create mistakes in lesson materials requiring double-checking, or even “correct already correct student writing” [4]. More fundamentally, teachers worry about students’ over-reliance on AI, which they fear could hinder critical thinking and creativity. Academic dishonesty—students generating entire assignments with AI—is a related concern [5].

    Data privacy adds another ethical dimension: teachers recognize the need to balance personalized learning with respecting student privacy, ensuring transparent policies, and mitigating algorithmic bias [4].

    Need for Professional Development#

    Despite optimism, many teachers feel unprepared to integrate AI effectively. They report lacking clear guidance from their institutions and needing more than basic technical training—they need pedagogical strategies for designing AI-enhanced learning experiences [14]. The message is consistent: comprehensive professional development focused on AI literacy is essential, not optional [15].

    Maintaining Human Interaction and Teacher Creativity#

    Some teachers worry that over-reliance on AI could diminish the human interaction that is central to language development. There’s also a more personal concern: that because AI can so readily provide content, teachers themselves might become less creative or, as one study put it, “lazy” [4].

    This points to a crucial distinction. The dialogic agent role of AI [3] is genuinely valuable—but it operates within a larger pedagogical ecosystem where human teachers remain irreplaceable for fostering deeper understanding, emotional intelligence, and the complex communicative competence that extends beyond linguistic accuracy.

    What the Literature Gap Reveals#

    One of the paper’s most important contributions is identifying what the literature has not yet examined. While existing research addresses educator perspectives on AI in ELT broadly, very few studies specifically investigate its role within dialogic learning contexts. The conceptual framework proposed here—AI as dialogic agent—is designed to generate exactly that kind of focused inquiry.

    The call to researchers: move beyond general AI-in-ELT studies to examine the nuanced impacts of AI as a collaborative partner specifically within dialogic learning pedagogies. This matters because the dialogic context places particular demands on AI—demands for conversational authenticity, responsiveness, and the ability to facilitate rather than terminate dialogue—that haven’t yet been systematically studied.

    Practical Implications for Teachers#

    The synthesis suggests a practical approach:

    Embrace AI’s safe-space function first. Use AI chatbots to lower anxiety around speaking practice before asking students to engage in more demanding human dialogues. The scaffold builds toward genuine dialogic competence.

    Design for autonomy, not dependency. Rather than having AI write for students, design tasks where students use AI for specific sub-tasks (generating ideas, checking grammar) while maintaining ownership of the intellectual work.

    Advocate for infrastructure and training. Teacher perceptions are positive, but readiness is low. The implementation gap between enthusiasm and effective use can only be closed with institutional support: reliable infrastructure, clear policies, and ongoing professional development.

    Stay human at the center. AI can handle what AI does well—personalization, feedback, practice opportunities. The teacher’s irreplaceable role is in the higher-order work: facilitating critical reasoning, building relationships, navigating the ethical and emotional dimensions of learning that AI cannot address.
