    🔬 When Open-Source AI Meets Qualitative Research: Trust, Privacy, and the Limits of LLM-Assisted Coding

    March 16, 2026

    Based on research by Tung T. Ngo, Dai Nguyen Van, Anh-Minh Nguyen, Phuong-Anh Do, and Anh Nguyen-Quoc, 2026

    Qualitative data analysis is one of the most intellectually demanding tasks in the social sciences. Open coding — the painstaking process of reading through interview transcripts, field notes, or documents and assigning conceptual labels to segments of meaning — can take weeks or months for even a modestly sized dataset. So when large language models (LLMs) started showing real capability at language tasks, qualitative researchers understandably took notice. The question isn’t whether AI can speed up this work; it’s whether researchers can trust it enough to actually use it, and whether doing so compromises the rigour that makes qualitative research valuable in the first place.

    This paper, presented at CHI EA ‘26 in Barcelona, tackles that question head-on [1]. The team introduced ChatQDA, an on-device qualitative analysis tool powered by open-source LLMs, and ran a mixed-methods user study to understand how researchers actually experience working with it. What they found is nuanced, sometimes counterintuitive, and practically important for anyone thinking about how to build AI tools for sensitive research contexts.

    The Privacy Problem with Commercial LLMs#

    Most conversation about AI-assisted qualitative analysis has centred on tools like ChatGPT or Gemini. These are powerful, widely accessible, and familiar — but they come with a serious problem for qualitative researchers: your data leaves your machine [2]. When the data involves interviews with vulnerable populations, sensitive organisational information, or anything covered by an institutional review board (IRB) protocol, uploading transcripts to a third-party API isn’t just ethically uncomfortable; it may be outright impermissible.

    Commercial LLMs also carry the documented risk that sensitive inputs can be inadvertently memorised by the model and potentially reproduced later [3]. For qualitative researchers working with confidential material, this is a hard constraint, not a matter of preference.

    Open-source LLMs — models like Llama, DeepSeek-R1, or the gpt-oss series — offer a different path. Deployed locally or within institution-controlled infrastructure, they process data without it ever leaving a secure boundary [4]. They can also be adapted to domain-specific language through techniques like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [5] or grounded in a researcher’s own corpus via Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) to reduce hallucinations [6].

    The catch is that open-source models introduce their own barriers: configuration complexity, command-line interfaces, quantization settings, and a general assumption of technical fluency that most qualitative researchers simply do not have [7].

    ChatQDA: An On-Device Solution#

    The authors’ response to this tension is ChatQDA, built on Hugging Face’s Chat UI framework. It uses a conversational interface — deliberately chosen because researchers are already comfortable with tools like ChatGPT — and allows users to upload documents and query them directly. Chat history is embedded in the prompt dynamically, so the model can refine its outputs based on ongoing feedback within a session.

    The underlying model is gpt-oss-20b [8], a 20.9-billion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model that uses MXFP4 quantization to fit within a 16GB RAM footprint. It activates only around 3.6 billion parameters per inference pass, keeping latency low while remaining competitive on reasoning benchmarks. Crucially for the privacy argument: it runs entirely on the researcher’s own hardware.

    The Study: Who Used It and How#

    The user study followed a mixed-methods design [9]. The team recruited four participants across two groups: novice qualitative researchers (fewer than two years of experience, limited AI exposure) and more experienced researchers (at least two years of QDA experience, some prior AI use). Small sample size is typical for this kind of exploratory HCI study — the goal is depth of insight, not statistical generalisation.

    Each participant completed two tasks using the same 5-page semi-structured interview transcript:

    1. Manual open coding (baseline), following their usual procedures
    2. ChatQDA-assisted open coding for one additional hour

    Afterwards, participants filled out a post-task survey using validated 5-point Likert scales measuring perceived security, privacy, ease of use, usefulness, and trust [10], [11], [12]. This was followed by an approximately 40-minute semi-structured interview, audio-recorded and transcribed using OpenAI Whisper [13].

    Analysis combined descriptive statistics on survey responses with deductive thematic analysis of interview transcripts, guided by the same constructs measured in the survey.

    Key Findings#

    Security: Epistemic Uncertainty, Not Distrust#

    Security ratings clustered around the neutral midpoint (Median = 3). This might look like indifference, but the qualitative data tells a more interesting story. Participants weren’t expressing distrust of the system — they were expressing uncertainty about their own ability to evaluate it. One participant put it plainly: “Although I’m a qualitative researcher, I’m not a technology engineer… I don’t really trust that this platform can prevent a third party from modifying the data I process. I’m not sure.”

    This is a critical design insight: invisible security measures, however technically robust, do not produce felt security. Local deployment is a necessary but not sufficient condition for user confidence.

    Privacy: The Theory-Practice Gap#

    Privacy ratings were more elevated (most items clustering at 3–4), with highest concern around data collection during use (75% agreement; M = 3.75) and potential secondary use of research data (M = 3.50). Participants understood the theoretical case for open-source privacy but could not verify it independently. As one noted: “In theory, open-source LLMs may protect user data better than closed ones, but in practice we can’t know how developers use the information, or whether an open-source model contains malicious code to take user data.”

    The gap between theoretical privacy benefit and practical verifiability is a real problem that good interface design needs to close — not just assume away.

    Ease of Use: Accessible Entry, Uncertain Mastery#

    Ease of use scores were consistently positive. All participants rated 4 (agree) for learnability and clarity of interaction. Perceived ability to become skillful was more mixed (M = 3.00), suggesting that while getting started felt approachable, participants were less confident about developing deeper proficiency.

    The qualitative data explains the initial accessibility well: prompting ChatQDA feels natural to anyone who has used a conversational AI tool. Novice users found it straightforward to iterate on prompts, adjusting keywords and instructions to progressively refine outputs. This is genuinely good news for the adoption case.

    Usefulness: Speed Yes, Quality Uncertain#

    Perceived usefulness was positive but differentiated. The strongest endorsement was for speed (50% rated 5; M = 3.75, Md = 4). Overall usefulness and performance improvement showed more variability. The pattern makes intuitive sense: ChatQDA is most clearly useful as a time-saver for preliminary processing. Whether it actually improves the quality of analytic outputs is a harder question, and participants were appropriately uncertain.

    Trustworthiness: Conditional and Task-Dependent#

    The most theoretically rich finding concerns trust. Overall trustworthiness ratings clustered around neutral (M = 3.00), with perceived reputation lowest (M = 2.75) and competence/effectiveness somewhat higher (M = 3.25). But averages mask the important pattern: trust varied sharply by sub-task.

    Participants were reasonably comfortable with excerpt extraction — pulling relevant passages from text. They were much less comfortable with labelling and first-order coding, where they observed hallucination and inconsistency. One participant described the problem vividly: “It identifies only 8 excerpts, but when assigning first-order codes it becomes 10. Usually it assigns one code per excerpt; the AI cannot identify meaning-based relationships across excerpts to group them under the same code.”

    Prompt sensitivity was a separate concern. When instructions changed, outputs changed significantly — unpredictably so. “Consistency is only slightly above average. When I change the instructions, the results change immediately, and that can push the outputs further apart.”

    The system handled surface-level meaning well. It struggled with latent meaning, indirect expressions, and interpretations requiring broader contextual understanding across the dataset.

    Design Recommendations#

    The paper translates these findings into three actionable design recommendations for future qualitative analysis tools.

    Make Privacy and Security Visible#

    Technical privacy is not the same as experienced privacy. Tools need to make the security posture legible to non-technical users through:

    • Clear in-app statements about what is stored, logged, and how to delete it
    • A persistent “local-only / no network calls” indicator
    • Model provenance information (version, source, checksum) to address concerns about open-source supply chain risks [14]

    Build Trust through Traceability and Consistency#

    Conditional trust — trusting the tool for extraction but not for coding — is rational given current capabilities. Designs should support rather than undermine this calibrated scepticism by:

    • Forcing evidence-based outputs with highlighted spans and line references for every excerpt and code
    • Adding guardrails to prevent new excerpts or codes from appearing across analytic stages
    • Enabling reproducible runs through saved prompts, settings, and lockable codebooks
    • Supporting grouping of related excerpts under shared codes, addressing the consistency problem directly

    Move from “Fast” to “Methodologically Defensible”#

    The efficiency benefit is real and valued. But efficiency alone is not enough to justify adoption for researchers who care deeply about analytic rigour. The next generation of tools should integrate memoing and audit trails, link codes to evidence in the corpus, and provide lightweight prompt templates calibrated to common qualitative tasks — not just to make users competent, but to make them genuinely skilful [15], [16].

    What This Means in Practice#

    The ChatQDA study is a useful corrective to two kinds of hype. The first is the hype around AI as a wholesale replacement for qualitative judgment — the study shows clearly that experienced researchers do not want this and do not trust it. The second is the hype around open-source deployment as a straightforward privacy solution — the study shows that technical privacy and experienced privacy are different things, and the gap between them needs active design work.

    The more productive framing is that tools like ChatQDA can serve as capable assistants for the surface-level, time-consuming parts of qualitative analysis, freeing up researcher bandwidth for the interpretive work that genuinely requires human judgment. Getting there requires transparency about what the model can and cannot do, verifiable privacy controls, and consistent, traceable outputs that researchers can audit and override.

    For qualitative researchers sitting on large datasets who have been reluctant to use commercial AI for ethical reasons, on-device open-source tools represent a real option — one that is still maturing, but developing in the right direction.
